Colorado AI Act Deadline June 30: What Real Estate Agents Must Know About AI Listing Compliance

📅 June 25, 2026 ⏱ 8 min read 🏠 Real Estate Compliance

If you're a real estate agent in Colorado who uses AI tools to write listing descriptions — or you're considering it — you need to know what changes on June 30.

That's when the Colorado AI Act (SB 24-205) begins enforcement. It's the first state-level law in the United States to regulate how artificial intelligence is used in decisions that affect people's lives. And real estate is explicitly covered.

Here's what's changing, how it affects your listings, and exactly what you need to do to stay compliant.

What the Colorado AI Act Does

SB 24-205 regulates the use of AI systems that make — or are a substantial factor in making — "consequential decisions." These are decisions with significant impact on individuals' lives, including housing, employment, education, finance, and healthcare.

If you use AI tools like ChatGPT, Jasper, or a dedicated listing generator to produce your MLS descriptions, you're using AI to influence a consequential decision. The law requires that:

  • Disclosure. You must notify individuals when AI is being used in a decision that affects them.
  • Right to appeal. Individuals must have a way to request an alternative decision or correction if an AI system produces an adverse outcome.
  • Transparency. You must be able to explain how your AI tool works and what data it uses.
  • Risk management. You need a governance framework to identify and mitigate risks of algorithmic discrimination.
"Housing is a consequential decision under Colorado law. AI-generated listing descriptions that influence someone's housing choices fall squarely within the scope of SB 24-205."

Why This Matters for Real Estate Listings

On its surface, writing a listing description with AI seems low-risk. But the Colorado Attorney General's office has made it clear that any AI system that shapes consequential decisions — even indirectly — must comply.

A listing description influences which properties potential buyers see, how properties are presented, and what information is surfaced or omitted. If your AI tool generates copy that includes discriminatory language, or if you use AI-generated listings without disclosure, you're exposed on two fronts:

Let's separate the two issues — because they're related but distinct.

Issue 1: The $26,262 Fair Housing Risk

This isn't new, but it's worth repeating. The Fair Housing Act prohibits discrimination in housing transactions on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, disability, familial status, and national origin. The maximum civil penalty for a first violation is $26,262 — and it jumps to $65,659 for subsequent violations.

AI tools make this risk different, not less. Because AI models can reproduce subtle discriminatory patterns from their training data, they may generate listing copy with biased language you wouldn't catch unless you're specifically looking for it. Terms like "perfect for families," "retirement community," "starter home," or "walking distance to church" are common AI outputs that can trigger Fair Housing complaints.

The Colorado AI Act adds a second layer: if your AI tool produces discriminatory output, you're not just facing a Fair Housing penalty — you may also face an additional enforcement action under SB 24-205 for failing to manage algorithmic discrimination risk.

Issue 2: The Disclosure Requirement (New)

Starting June 30, if you use AI to produce listing descriptions, you need a disclosure statement on every listing. Colorado law doesn't prescribe the exact language, but it must clearly inform readers that AI was used in the content they're viewing.

Here's the disclosure template we recommend:

📋 Recommended AI Disclosure Statement
"This listing description was generated with the assistance of artificial intelligence and reviewed by a licensed real estate professional. Required by Colorado AI Act, SB 24-205."

Place this at the bottom of every AI-assisted listing in Colorado. It's short, honest, and meets the transparency requirement.

What You Need to Do Before June 30

If you're a Colorado agent or brokerage, here's your compliance checklist:

  1. Audit your tools. Identify every AI tool you use to generate, edit, or optimize listing descriptions.
  2. Add disclosure statements. Include the AI disclosure on every active and future AI-assisted listing.
  3. Review your listings for Fair Housing violations. Run every existing AI-generated listing through a compliance check.
  4. Build an audit trail. Document which listings used AI, when, and what compliance steps were taken. This is your insurance if a complaint is filed.
  5. Train your agents. Every agent in your brokerage needs to know these rules. Make compliance part of your onboarding.
  6. Set up a risk management framework. Have a process for reviewing AI-generated content before it goes live. Automated checking + human review = best practice.

What Happens If You Don't Comply?

The Colorado Attorney General can bring enforcement actions under SB 24-205, including civil penalties of up to $10,000 per violation, injunctive relief, and corrective action requirements. Combine that with a Fair Housing complaint — which can add a $26,262 penalty per violation — and the financial exposure adds up quickly.

Beyond the fines: homeowners and prospective buyers can use the AI Act's right to appeal to challenge decisions they believe were influenced by biased or improper AI use. Even if a claim doesn't result in a penalty, the professional liability and reputational damage are significant.

"Colorado is the first state to enforce AI-specific regulations on housing decisions. But it won't be the last. California and Texas have bills in committee. What starts in Denver won't stay in Denver."

The Bottom Line

The Colorado AI Act raises the bar on how AI is used in real estate. The disclosure requirement is straightforward — a single sentence at the bottom of each listing. The Fair Housing compliance requirement is something every agent should already be doing. And the risk management framework is simply good business practice.

None of this is complicated. But it's mandatory. And June 30 is coming.
